The word "digital product" still makes some people think: I would need to learn to code for that.

You do not.

The tools available today let you create, package, and sell digital products with no technical background. The barrier is not skill. It is knowing which tools to use for what.

## What People Think Is Required vs. What Actually Is

**What people think:** a website, a developer, a custom checkout, maybe a course platform, some kind of CRM, and a payment processor that requires API knowledge.

**What you actually need:** something to write or design your product in, somewhere to host and sell it, and a way to take payment. For most digital products, that is three tools, and all three have free tiers.

The mental model most people start with is borrowed from software companies. You are not building a software company. You are building a guide, a template, or a resource that someone downloads and uses.

## The Real Question

The question is not "what technical skills do I need?" It is "what is the simplest combination of tools that gets this product in front of a buyer?"

For a PDF guide: write it in Google Docs, design a cover in Canva, sell it through Gumroad or Lemon Squeezy. That is the full stack. No code. No monthly fees beyond what you already use. No technical knowledge beyond being able to click through a setup wizard.

For a template: build it in Notion, Airtable, or Google Sheets, duplicate it for delivery, sell it the same way.

For a checklist: even simpler.

### Where People Get Stuck

The confusion usually comes from trying to find the perfect tool before starting. There are dozens of platforms, and comparing them endlessly feels like research but it is actually procrastination with better branding.

Pick the simplest tool for your product type. Start there. You can always switch later once you have something worth switching.

## Three Decisions That Get You Moving

**1. What format does your product take?**

PDF, template, spreadsheet, Notion doc? Match the format to the type of value you are delivering. If it is a process, a guide or checklist works. If it is a system, a template works.

**2. Where will you sell it?**

For most first-time creators, a platform like Gumroad or Lemon Squeezy handles payment, delivery, and basic product hosting in one place. You do not need a custom website to start.

**3. What does the buyer receive after they pay?**

Usually a download link. Sometimes a link to a Notion page or a Google Doc template they can duplicate. Either way, this is simpler to set up than it sounds.

## What You Still Need to Figure Out

The tools are the easy part. The harder part is making a product that people actually want to buy and knowing how to position it so the right person understands what it is for.

Technical setup takes an afternoon. Getting the product right takes more thought. That is where the real work is.
